# Product Strategy

TRNAWL exists because operational coordination still fails even when companies already have many tools.

Most companies have Jira, ServiceNow, Planner, Excel, Teams, email, and SharePoint. The gap is not another generic task system. The gap is execution visibility across delivery, sales, architecture, governance, and customer coordination.

## What TRNAWL Is

TRNAWL is a lightweight operational workflow and accountability platform.

It helps teams answer practical execution questions:

- Who owns this?
- What is blocked?
- Is delivery involved?
- Is pricing approved?
- Did legal review it?
- Are assumptions clear?
- Is the customer waiting on us?
- Which actions are becoming stale?

## What TRNAWL Is Not

TRNAWL should not become:

- Agile software planning
- Sprint management
- Enterprise BPM
- ITSM replacement
- Jira clone
- ServiceNow clone
- Monday.com clone
- Full project management suite

The strength of TRNAWL is lightweight governance, operational execution, presales/delivery coordination, and AI-assisted clarity.

## Core Philosophy

### Fast Enough That People Actually Use It

If updating a ticket feels slower than sending a Teams message, adoption will fail.

The product should stay:

- Lightweight
- Fast
- Drag-and-drop
- Low-friction
- Minimal on required fields

### Operational Governance Without Bureaucracy

TRNAWL should provide the minimum operational workflow needed to prevent common execution gaps.

For a new SOW, that can mean:

- Delivery review
- Pricing review
- Legal review
- Scope boundary review
- Customer deck review

This is not mandatory bureaucracy. It is smart structure that makes critical handoffs visible.

### AI As A Governance Layer

AI should improve operational quality, not behave like a generic chatbot.

During ticket creation, AI can:

- Rephrase unclear input
- Structure the request
- Suggest an owner or review group
- Suggest a due date
- Identify missing information
- Flag operational risk

Later, AI can review active work for:

- Unrealistic assumptions
- Hidden scope gaps
- Staffing risks
- SLA conflicts
- Stale tickets
- Delayed reviews
- Overloaded team members

## Most Valuable Feature

The most valuable feature is workflows.

Workflows capture organizational experience and turn it into repeatable execution logic. A New SOW Workflow should create the minimum review tasks needed to move from intake to customer-ready delivery plan without forgotten handoffs.

## Near-Term Product Additions

### Operational Readiness Score

Each SOW or delivery workflow should show a lightweight readiness score based on visible checks:

- Delivery reviewed
- Pricing reviewed
- Scope boundaries clear
- Owner assigned
- Customer dependencies listed
- Due dates realistic

The first version can be rule-based. AI can later explain why a score is weak and suggest what is missing.

### Blocked Because

Every blocked ticket should use a controlled reason so reports become useful:

- Waiting for delivery
- Waiting for pricing
- Waiting for legal
- Waiting for customer
- Scope unclear
- Owner missing
- Dependency unresolved
- Approval pending

This is low-friction for users and high-value for operations.

### Customer Action Register

Customer-owned actions need a dedicated view because they often disappear in email or meeting notes.

The register should track:

- Customer
- Action
- Customer owner
- Internal accountable person
- Due date
- Status
- Days waiting

### Weekly Operational Brief

TRNAWL should generate a weekly brief from real data:

- SOWs waiting for delivery review
- Pricing reviews overdue
- Customer actions stale
- Owners overloaded next week
- Tickets blocked by unclear scope

This should be management-ready, but short.

## Calendar Direction

The calendar should not become MS Project or heavy resource forecasting.

It should answer one simple question:

Who is blocked, reserved, or overloaded, and when?

Drag-and-drop capacity planning is enough for the first versions.

## Growth Path

### Phase 1

Simple operational tracker for internal use:

- Ticket board
- Owners
- Due dates
- Blocked reasons
- Delivery review workflow
- New SOW workflow
- Customer action register
- AI ticket enrichment
- Basic dashboard

### Phase 2

Cross-team coordination:

- Workflow templates
- Team capacity calendar
- Reports
- Readiness scores
- Weekly operational briefs
- Saved views
- Teams and Outlook capture

### Phase 3

AI governance layer:

- Risk detection
- Stale work detection
- Delivery assumption review
- Staffing and SLA conflict checks
- Operational intelligence summaries

Example outputs:

- These SOWs likely miss delivery assumptions.
- Pricing review is delayed by 6 days.
- Thomas is overloaded next week.
- This customer has unresolved actions.

## Product Guardrails

- Keep the first version simple.
- Prefer adoption over configuration depth.
- Use workflows to encode operational knowledge.
- Use AI to improve accountability and clarity.
- Avoid building a generic PM suite.
- Avoid turning every process into heavy administration.
